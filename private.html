The Complete Guide to Maximum Security Online

Learn how you can stay anonymous to protect your privacy while browsing the net.


Emilia Ton

1. Unsafe web browsing practices

There are often signs that sites you visit can be malicious and ask for your PI: free offers, shortened URLs, pages socially engineered to trick users to set up an account and download malware from them.

To Do List includes:

  • Keep your antivirus up to date. Always scan the files before you download them.
  • Don’t reuse passwords for multiple websites. Use strong and secure passwords and keep them in a password management system.
  • Use secure browsers and avoid the unverified ones. Only use trusted add-ons and extensions.
  • Always remember to set up two factor authentication to protect your information.

2. Cookies and web tracking

Cookies are files downloaded to your browser by a website that contain unique identifier data about the site. When a website "sees" the data it sets in a cookie, it knows the browser is one that has contacted it before. Cookies can also be used to track your activities and capture your purchasing habits and then be shared with unwanted third parties affiliated with the site.

To Do List includes:

  • Consider switching from your default search engines to engines that don't track you like DuckDuckGo or StartPage.
  • Set your browser to delete cookies every time you finish browsing.
  • Set "opt out" cookies on your browser to cookies that aren't allowed at all in your browser.

3. IP address tracking

The COPPA Act specifically states that IP addresses are personal information since they are information about an identifiable individual associated with them. An Internet Protocol (IP) address is a numerical label behind the familiar web addresses we see every day. It identifies a device over the internet. Hackers often come through IP addresses as their first point of attack.

To Do List includes:

  • When working on your personal computer, you can use a Virtual Private Network (VPN) tool. However, avoid free ones or proxies and pay a small amount for the highly rated ones like NordVPN or CyberGhost VPN.
  • Employees or clients at home have “leased” IP addresses with their cable modem and ISP accounts. Your IP won’t change until you turn off your modem. Power it down as often as you feel the need.
  • If you set up a website, you can request a private WHOIS listing from the database manager, Network Solutions.

4. Using HTTP Instead of HTTPS Encrypted Web Server Connections

Personal data flowing between a user's machine and a website using plain HTTP protocol can be monitored by other companies or potentially intercepted and stolen by malicious hackers (often called the "man-in-the-middle"). That’s where Secure Sockets Layer(SSL) comes in.

To Do List includes:

  • When purchasing or entering personal information on websites, always check for an “https://” or a padlock icon in your browser’s URL bar to verify that a site is secure before entering any personal information.
  • If you're hosting a website, consider implementing SSL on your web server to ensure data privacy between you and customers.

5. The threat from the cloud

Cloud computing is the latest and greatest technological wave that brings up new issues for data privacy. This is especially true when you give up administrative and technological controls to an outside party. A cloud provider may be deficient in backup processes, security practices, employee controls, application interfaces & APIs and more!

To Do List includes:

  • Ensure the provider has all your data that is stored with them encrypted.
  • Don’t share private information or grant access to your pictures or locations while using any online services like social media.
  • Enable self-security logging and monitoring to monitor any unauthorized access attempts and other issues.
